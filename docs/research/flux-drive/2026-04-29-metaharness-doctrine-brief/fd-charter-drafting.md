## Summary

A charter drafting specialist reads this brief as a proposal to create foundational documents without first resolving the authority-level hierarchy: which document is constitutional (permanent, constraining, requires formal amendment), which is bylaw-level (operational, amendable by practice change), and which is policy-level (local, revisable by the current maintainer). The proposed spine names four documents but does not assign authority levels, and the proposed mission sentence carries implementation-specific terms that will require amendment within the lifespan of V1.

## Operational pattern in source domain

Constitutional drafting separates documents by **authority level**. A constitution states why an entity exists and what it may never do — it contains no implementation detail because implementation detail has a shorter lifespan than the constitution itself. Bylaws translate constitutional purpose into operational rules: standing committees, decision procedures, quorum requirements. Policies translate bylaw rules into current practice: how the treasurer files this year's receipts. The charter drafter's discipline is: if a claim will require amendment when the implementation changes, it does not belong in the constitution. The corollary: a constitutional negative ("the legislature shall make no law abridging...") must be scoped precisely enough to constrain future decisions without blocking legitimate evolution. An over-broad negative ("Nousromancer shall not absorb Sylveste") can block legitimate adapter evolution as much as an under-scoped one.

## Findings

- **P1 — The proposed mission sentence (line 80) contains implementation-specific terms that will require constitutional amendment as integrations evolve.** The sentence names "Beads," "CASS," "handoff packets," "review/receipt integrations" as part of the vision description at line 75 and implicitly in the mission sentence framing. "Beads" and "CASS" are V1 integration artifacts, not permanent architectural primitives. If `MISSION.md` or `VISION.md` names these directly, the document will require formal amendment each time an integration term changes. Concrete failure: a future agent is uncertain whether renaming "Beads" to a new artifact ID requires a `MISSION.md` amendment (treating it as constitutional) or only an `AGENTS.md` update (treating it as bylaw). The ambiguity causes either over-formality (every artifact rename triggers a mission revision) or under-formality (mission drift as terms change without revision).

- **P1 — The three-layer identity (lines 64–67) is stated in the doctrine brief but not translated into a bylaw-level decision rule in any proposed document.** The constitutional structure states the layers. But no proposed bylaw-equivalent document (`AGENTS.md` or `CLAUDE.md`) is specified to translate the layer boundary into an operational decision rule: "Feature X belongs on the public layer if [condition]; on the operator layer if [condition]." Without this bylaw translation, the constitutional layer claim is unenforceable. A future agent inherits the constitution but has no bylaws. Concrete failure: a new contributor reads `MISSION.md`, sees three layers, and assigns a new attention-state widget to the public layer because no bylaw document says otherwise — exactly the layer-collapse the brief intends to prevent.

- **P2 — The six constraints in the "Constraints / boundaries" section (lines 89–96) are mixed-authority-level and unsorted.** A charter drafter categorizes these: constraints 1 and 2 ("Do not make Nousromancer Sylveste itself," "Do not import Sylveste canon") are constitutional negatives — permanent, high-authority, amendment-resistant. Constraint 3 ("Do not erase the public extension identity") is constitutional. Constraint 4 ("Do not overclaim attention triage") is bylaw-level — it governs current implementation policy and will evolve as upstream attention-state fields mature. Constraints 5 and 6 are policy-level operational guidelines. Mixing these in a flat list in the doctrine brief means they will be copied into root canon at the wrong authority level: constitutional constraints buried next to policy guidelines, policy guidelines elevated to constitutional status.

- **P2 — The PHILOSOPHY.md will become a cathedral if anti-goals specific to V1 are written at constitutional level.** Lines 72 and 96 correctly note the anti-bloat risk. But the charter drafter's specific concern is: anti-goals in `PHILOSOPHY.md` that name current scope (e.g., "no authoritative `needs input`") are V1 policy, not constitutional principle. Writing them in `PHILOSOPHY.md` as principles creates a document that requires amendment each time the scope of attention-state authority evolves.

- **P3 — Reviewer question 5 (line 104) conflates mission-level and implementation-level concerns.** "What should be the next concrete doc/bead after review: mission interview, architecture doc, roadmap, or implementation plan?" A charter drafter would split this: the next mission-level artifact is the mission interview (constitutional); the architecture doc is bylaw-level; the roadmap and implementation plan are policy-level. These are sequential, not alternatives. Offering them as a choice suggests the brief does not yet have a clear authority-level hierarchy for the document creation process itself.

## Recommendations

- **Line 80 (mission sentence):** Strip all V1 artifact names. Replace "Beads, CASS recall, handoff packets, review state, route decisions, receipts" with the constitutional abstraction: "agent work artifacts, state transitions, and human-judgment events." The mission sentence should remain true if every artifact is renamed or replaced. Proposed revision: "Nousromancer exists to make Hermes Agent a usable metaharness for complex agent work: a live operator surface that shows which agents and workflows need human judgment, why they matter now, and how to answer without collapsing external system canon into Hermes."
- **Lines 64–67 (layered identity):** Add a mandate to the brief that `AGENTS.md` (not `MISSION.md`) must contain a decision table: one row per layer, with "Feature belongs here if:" conditions in plain language. This is the bylaw translation of the constitutional layer claim.
- **Lines 89–96 (constraints):** Re-sort by authority level before writing root canon. Label constraints 1–3 as [CONSTITUTIONAL] (appear in `MISSION.md` or `PHILOSOPHY.md`, require formal revision). Label constraint 4 as [BYLAW] (appear in `AGENTS.md`, amendment by maintainer consensus). Label constraints 5–6 as [POLICY] (appear in `AGENTS.md` or `CLAUDE.md`, amendment by current practice).
- **Lines 71–76 (root canon spine):** Add one sentence to the brief specifying the authority hierarchy: "`MISSION.md` > `PHILOSOPHY.md` > `VISION.md` > `AGENTS.md`/`CLAUDE.md`. Claims in higher-authority documents constrain lower-authority documents; amendment requires explicit revision of the higher-authority document."

## What this lens cannot see

This lens has nothing meaningful to say about the truthfulness of live attention signals, the feed-promotion rules, or the on-air/off-air state discipline — that is broadcast control room territory. It cannot evaluate whether "metaharness" requires a controlled-vocabulary definition in the public catalog. It also cannot assess the diplomatic risk of naming Sylveste in public documents — that requires the diplomatic protocol lens. The question of whether `AGENTS.md` is an authority record extension or a local practice note is catalog-governance territory, though the authority-level assignment (bylaw vs. policy) is within charter-drafting scope.
